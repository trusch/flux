NEBULA

flux in three dimensions

A real fluid simulation — but this time the water is space itself, and you are made of it. A 64×64×64 Navier-Stokes solver runs on your GPU, volume-raymarched into glowing nebulae. The player is a cloud of ink held together by an invisible force pulling it toward your heart — accelerate and your body trails behind you like a comet; coast and it gathers itself back. The planets aren't decoration: their gravity bends your path AND tears ink from your body as you pass. Fall onto one and you are consumed — and reborn.

D-pad steers your heart, thrust accelerates, brake below it. Drag the screen to look. Double-tap detonates your own body outward — the binding force gathers it back. Skim a planet to feel gravity steal your ink; fight free or fall.

The origin

Part of a single-prompt AI showcase series that began with FLUX. This one came from:

"I want also a fluid simulation but it should be in 3d and the player is able to move the fluid in space… and we need some spheres in the space like planets or stars so we have something we are relative to, so we see the movement."

Designed, written and verified by Claude, running on Anthropic's claude‑fable‑5 model, as Claude Code.

Under the hood

One self-contained HTML file, zero libraries. The 3D grid lives as a tiled 2D atlas (64³ → 512×512, manual trilinear sampling with toroidal wrap), so every solver pass — semi-Lagrangian advection, divergence, 8 Jacobi pressure iterations, gradient subtraction, wake splatting — is an ordinary fullscreen fragment pass. The display pass raymarches the dye volume (emission + absorption, jittered steps) and ray-traces twelve analytic spheres with a procedural starfield behind. Resolution adapts to hold frame rate. Audio: an engine that follows your speed, thrust and brake gestures, distance bells — raw oscillators and shaped noise through a runtime convolution reverb.
